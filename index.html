A frontier church, put permanently into type.

For my father, Dr. James Sink

Every book this old is a traveler. This one started in Fayetteville, Tennessee, in 1827: a little press, a frontier church with more conviction than credentials. By 1880 it was in Austin, Texas, where an owner loved it enough to write its story inside the front cover. Then the trail goes quiet. Years of shelves and hands nobody recorded, until it surfaced in front of me, and I knew where it was going next.

The man who wrote it came to his convictions the hard way. No seminary, no license handed to him, just years of working it out for himself. And what he wrote is an argument for exactly that kind of faith: his church had broken away over a single conviction — that no one is born already lost, that grace is offered to everyone, that faith is not fate but a choice. That made me think of you and the way you came to your faith: examined, questioned, chosen, and kept.

It isn't finished traveling. Someday it will sit on a shelf neither of us can picture. That's why the owner in Austin wrote its story inside the cover, so the next hands would know what they were holding. This page is me doing the same. This stop is yours.

Will

Introduction

What you are looking at is a book that is nearly two hundred years old, printed in a small Tennessee town for a church that was, at the time, barely a generation into existing at all. 1, 2Its full title, set in the careful type of its own title page, is A Series of Lectures, on the Most Important Subjects in Divinity, written by the Reverend Finis Ewing. 1It was not a private undertaking. The book was printed for the Cumberland Presbyterian Synod itself — the church's own governing body stood behind it and sent it out as official teaching, not one minister's personal opinion. 3That is what makes it remarkable: not simply its age, but what its existence quietly proves — that a young, scattered frontier church believed the printed word could hold a shared faith together across distances that preaching and memory alone could not reach. Meeting it here, on its own terms, is meeting the moment that church chose to put what it believed into permanent form and trust it to travel.

For my father, Dr. James Sink — Father's Day, June 21, 2026.

Why this book matters

This is not simply an old volume of sermons. It is denominational infrastructure — the paper-and-ink version of a young institution building itself a foundation. The 1827 Lectures were printed for the Cumberland Presbyterian Synod, not for Finis Ewing alone, which means the church's own governing body stood behind these words and circulated them as its teaching. 3

That teaching needed to travel because the church itself had been born out of a disagreement about it. When the frontier Cumberland ministers broke from the stricter Kentucky Synod, the dispute was over how ministers should be trained and how strictly they had to subscribe to the Westminster Confession: the Kentucky Synod wanted classical education and strict subscription, while the Cumberland ministers wanted room for less formally educated candidates and a more qualified assent. 4A denomination founded on a disagreement over doctrine needed, more than most, some way to make sure its own preachers and congregations were teaching the same thing.

By one account, these lectures grew directly out of Ewing's own theological school, where he had trained ministerial candidates in person, years before the church had seminaries to do that work for it. 5Printing them let that same teaching reach preachers and congregations who would never sit in his classroom.

That is the plain argument this exhibit makes: before this church had railroads to move its people, seminaries to train its ministers, or a settled bureaucracy to keep everyone aligned, it had a Synod-sponsored book. Doctrine, standardized and set in type, is how a scattered frontier movement held itself together and stayed one church instead of many.

Historical context · America in 1827

America in 1827

When the Cumberland Presbyterian Synod sent Finis Ewing's Lectures to the Fayetteville press, the country was governed by John Quincy Adams, in the late-Adams, pre-Jacksonian United States. 6Adams's third annual message, delivered that December, described a nation at peace and generally prosperous, its attention turned to commerce, the public debt, diplomacy, and military affairs on the frontier. 6, 7Much of the message dwelt on the unglamorous machinery of nation-building — roads, canals, and harbor surveys meant to bind a spreading country together. 6

That binding-together still happened the old way. On February 28, 1827, the Baltimore and Ohio Railroad received its charter. 8Construction did not begin until July 4, 1828 — a full year after Ewing's book had already come off the press. 9The Lectures therefore belonged to a world just before the rail era: whatever copies reached congregations in Missouri, Kentucky, or elsewhere still traveled by road, river, and wagon. 9

Print itself, though, was already becoming one of American Protestantism's chief instruments. 10By the mid-1820s the American Bible Society was a mature, annual-report-producing institution, the American Tract Society had organized in New York just two years earlier, and the American Sunday-School Union was building its own catalog of religious instruction. 10No record yet shows Cumberland Presbyterian leaders consciously modeling their program on these national societies, but the Synod's decision to sponsor a full doctrinal book for its frontier ministers moved with the same current running through American religious life — the conviction that a printed page could travel where a preacher could not, and hold a church's teaching steady across hundreds of miles. 10

The founder · Finis Ewing

Finis Ewing (1773–1841)

Finis Ewing was born July 10, 1773, in Bedford County, Virginia, and died July 4, 1841, in Lexington, Missouri.14, 16Between those two dates lies the life of a revival convert who became a founder, a preacher who became a teacher, and, eventually, an author — the man whose name is on the title page of the book this exhibit is built around.

His turn toward ministry began the way so much of the frontier's religious excitement did in that decade: in revival fire. Around 1798, Ewing and his wife experienced conversion under the preaching of the Rev. James McGready at the Red River meeting-house in Kentucky, one of the sparks of the wider western revival movement then sweeping the Cumberland region.17Five years later, in November 1803, he was ordained by the Transylvania Presbytery of the Presbyterian Church.20

That ordination did not settle him into a quiet pastorate. Within a few years, Ewing was one of three ministers — with Samuel McAdow and Samuel King — who broke from the stricter Kentucky Synod and, on February 4, 1810, constituted an independent Cumberland Presbytery at McAdow's log cabin near present-day Burns, Tennessee: the founding act of the Cumberland Presbyterian Church.24The break turned on how strictly a minister needed to be classically educated and how strictly he had to subscribe to the Westminster Confession of Faith — a dispute this exhibit examines in full in the section on the church itself, rather than here.4

Ewing married Margaret Davidson. Sources agree on that much, but disagree sharply on exactly when — three different dates appear across the record — so this exhibit states the marriage as a fact and leaves the date an open question rather than choosing among them.25

In May 1820, Ewing and his family left Christian County, Kentucky, and settled at New Lebanon, in what is now Cooper County, Missouri.12There he organized a new Cumberland Presbyterian congregation and, in his own home, opened a free theological school for ministerial candidates of McGee Presbytery — described by denominational sources as the first theological school west of the Mississippi River, though that particular claim still awaits fuller, independent confirmation.26He taught there through the early 1820s, alongside continued circuit preaching.13It was against that backdrop — a homemade frontier classroom, not a seminary — that Ewing produced the Lectures this exhibit documents; biographers describe the book as one of that school's direct fruits.5

A Conflict the Record Will Not Resolve

Here the story turns difficult, and this exhibit will not smooth it over.

The Cumberland Presbyterian Church's own historical foundation preserves a biography stating that Ewing resolved never to buy, sell, hold, or give away an enslaved person, and that he emancipated the people enslaved in his household at his death.27Independent, census-derived genealogical evidence reports something starkly different: federal census records list Ewing as holding seventeen enslaved people in Cooper County, Missouri, in 1830, and ten in Lafayette County, Missouri, in 1840.27

These are not the same kind of evidence, and it matters which is which. The census figures come from an original federal record made at the time — strong evidence, though with a complication of its own: before 1850, the census named only the head of a household, so the schedule alone cannot fully rule out that the person enumerated was a relative of the same name rather than Ewing himself.27The resolution-and-emancipation account, by contrast, comes from a denominational biography written to honor his memory — a kind of source more susceptible, by its very purpose, to idealizing the man it remembers.27

Nor are the two claims strictly incompatible on their face. A personal resolution and a later census record could both be true of the same life at different times — a pledge made and not kept, a resolution bounded to a period, or a memory that hardened into legend only after the fact.27Weighing what each source can and cannot bear, the evidence leans toward Ewing having been a recorded slaveholder, while the deeper question — how to reconcile that record with his own church's memory of him — remains genuinely, and properly, open.27

This exhibit does not close that gap, and no sentence here should be read as calling Ewing simply "anti-slavery," nor simply "an enslaver." Both accounts survive in the record, in tension, and only a will, a probate record, or a closer look at the original census pages could settle what neither can settle alone.27

Ewing died July 4, 1841, in Lexington, Missouri, and was buried at Machpelah Cemetery.15He left behind the church he had helped found three decades before, a Missouri classroom's worth of trained ministers, and this book — one preacher's teaching, set in type so it could keep circulating after he no longer could.

The church · Cumberland Presbyterians

The Cumberland Presbyterian Church

The church that printed this book was, in 1827, barely seventeen years old — younger than some of the ministers who served it. It had no seminary of its own yet, no settled bureaucracy, and congregations scattered across a moving frontier rather than gathered around a single city or synod headquarters. Understanding why that young, scattered church needed a printed book of doctrine at all starts with the disagreement that created it in the first place.

The Dispute That Made a New Church

The break came over two linked questions: how thoroughly a minister needed to be classically educated, and how strictly he had to subscribe to the Westminster Confession of Faith. The Kentucky Synod insisted on a fully classical, seminary-style education and strict, unqualified subscription to the Confession; the frontier ministers who would go on to found the Cumberland Presbyterian Church accepted candidates without that formal education and permitted a more qualified assent to the Confession's language.4It was, at heart, a dispute about who was allowed to preach and how tightly doctrine had to be held to before a man could be trusted with it — the exact question that made a Synod-sponsored, printed statement of that doctrine, years later, so useful to have.

February 4, 1810

That dispute came to a head at a specific place and a specific hour. On February 4, 1810, Finis Ewing, Samuel McAdow, and Samuel King constituted the independent Cumberland Presbytery at McAdow's own log cabin, near present-day Burns, in Dickson County, Tennessee — the founding act of what became the Cumberland Presbyterian Church.24One further name survives in the record of that day only as a nuance rather than a fourth founder: a man named Ephraim McLean is reported to have been present at the meeting, though he is not counted among the church's founders in the rest of the source record.24A denomination that would go on to ordain thousands began, that day, with three ministers and a borrowed cabin.

The Intermediate Plan

What that new church actually believed took a few more years to write down formally. In 1814, the Cumberland Presbyterians ratified their own Confession of Faith, adopted at Sugg's Creek Church, Tennessee — articulating what the Confession itself calls the "intermediate plan," a doctrinal position that rejected the strict Calvinist idea of eternal, unconditional reprobation while still retaining the perseverance of the saints.18It was, in its own words, a deliberate middle course — neither the old Kentucky Synod's stricter Calvinism nor its opposite — and it is that same "intermediate plan" language the 1814 Confession itself uses, not any later shorthand.18Ewing's own 1827 Lectures, delivered years afterward out of his own theological classroom, stand as one attempt to teach that intermediate plan systematically to a new generation of frontier ministers who would otherwise have had no seminary to learn it in.

From a Cabin to a General Assembly

A church built on qualified assent and frontier preaching grew fast. By 1829, the Cumberland Synod had reorganized into four constituent synods and established a full General Assembly — a national denominational structure, achieved within two decades of three men meeting in a borrowed cabin.21That growth is exactly why a Synod-sponsored book of lectures mattered as much as it did: a church expanding this quickly, across this much distance, could not simply trust that every new congregation had heard the same sermon from the same traveling preacher. It needed something that did not have to travel by horseback to be understood the same way twice.

A Church That Kept Testing Its Own Boundaries

The same frontier willingness to ordain men the established church would not — the willingness that had created the Cumberland Presbyterian Church in the first place — kept resurfacing as the denomination matured. In 1889, Nolin Presbytery ordained Louisa Mariah Layman Woosley, identified as the first woman ordained in any Presbyterian or Reformed denomination — a fresh instance of the same boundary-testing impulse that had once put unlettered frontier preachers into pulpits the Kentucky Synod had tried to close to them.22And the church's own identity was tested again from outside: in 1906, the Cumberland Presbyterian General Assembly voted to reunite with the Presbyterian Church in the U.S.A., a decision that split the denomination — some congregations rejoined the larger church, while others continued on as an independent Cumberland Presbyterian Church, and years of property litigation followed for those who stayed apart.23

A church this often tested — over who could preach, who could be ordained, and whether it should exist separately at all — is precisely the kind of institution for which a settled, printed, Synod-sponsored statement of belief was not a luxury. It was, in a very literal sense, how the Cumberland Presbyterian Church kept telling itself what it believed, long after the men who had founded it in a Tennessee cabin were gone.

The making · Printing & bookmaking

Printing & Bookmaking

The imprint on the title page reads plainly: printed in Fayetteville, Tennessee, by E. and J. B. Hill, 1827.1Behind those initials was very likely a pair of brothers — Ebenezer Hill and Joseph Bancroft Hill — though the identification, however strong, remains probable rather than settled.28

Ebenezer Hill had already spent years learning the printing trade in New England and New York before he came south, first to Alabama in 1819 and then to Fayetteville.28He was not a newcomer to the craft picking it up on the frontier; he arrived with it. In Fayetteville he built the town's first lasting newspaper, the Village Messenger, which began publishing in 1823.19That paper — and the wider Hill printing operation it anchored — is the most likely home for the type, the press, and the hands that made Ewing's book.

Fayetteville's print scene in the 1820s was young but not thin. Beyond the Village Messenger, the town's newspaper sequence over the following decade is remembered as including a paper called the Western Cabinet — though exactly when it began is not settled. One set of sources dates its start to 1829; another, to 1839.29Rather than quietly choosing one, this exhibit leaves the conflict visible: it is a small but honest reminder that even a well-documented print shop leaves gaps for a researcher to sort out later.

Making a 222-page book of theology in a county-seat print shop in 1827 meant hand-press printing on rag paper — cotton and linen rag remained the standard paper stock in America until wood pulp took over decades later.30Setting type letter by letter, inking the forme, and pulling each sheet through the press by hand, the Hill office turned Ewing's manuscript into an object meant to travel — to circuit riders, to congregations, to a denomination still finding its footing on the frontier.

What is not yet known matters too. Whether the Hills themselves stitched and covered the finished sheets, or whether the book left their shop as printed pages for someone else — a local binder, or the buyer — to bind, is not something the record currently settles. The craft of printing this book is traceable to Fayetteville with real confidence; the craft of binding it is, for now, an open question.

About this copy

About This Copy

Here is the honest part.

Almost everything this exhibit says about the 1827 Lectures as a physical object — its format, its binding, its paper — is drawn from a copy that is not this one. In 2026, another surviving 1827 copy came up for sale at auction, described as a small, 12mo volume of 222 pages, bound in original brown leather with a black leather label and gilt tooling, measuring roughly 6⅞ by 4⅜ inches.31That listing is the fullest physical description of this edition currently available — but it is a description written for a sale catalog, not a conservator's report, and it describes a different copy of the same book.31

Books of this age and kind were typically printed on paper made from cotton and linen rag, the common paper stock of the period, and a leather binding of the sort the auction copy shows would not be unusual for a book like this.30But "typical" and "unusual" are words about books in general — and until the summer of 2026, they were the only words this project could use about this book.

That changed on July 10, 2026, when this copy sat for its first photographs.32The camera confirmed what the auction copy could only suggest: the same modest brown leather, a red spine label lettered in gilt, the Fayetteville imprint at the foot of the title page.33, 34, 35And an earlier version of this very paragraph said that no one had yet looked for an inscription, a signature, or a stray pencil note in this copy. Someone has now — and the book answered. An early owner signed the title page in ink.36A later pencil hand marked it "First edition, 1827."37And on the front flyleaf, in period pencil, someone wrote a small memorial to Finis Ewing and to Rev. R. D. Morrow — remembered there as the first missionary the Cumberland Presbyterian Church ever sent out — and dated the page sideways along its edge: Austin, Texas, February 8th, 1880.38, 39The photographs and tentative transcriptions are in the gallery below; the readings are still unverified, and every name in them is now an open research question.

What remains unexamined is the finer bibliographer's work: the paper has not been studied for fiber, watermarks, or chain lines; the pages have not been counted and collated; the binding has not been formally assessed as original or later. That work is not a task hanging over this copy; it is the next visit to an old friend — made with clean, dry hands, no oils or polish on the leather, and a soft resting place when it isn't being read.40

This copy's photographs are in the gallery; the inspection notes and transcriptions live in the project's research log.

Primary sources

Primary Sources

Every sentence in this exhibit started life as a source before it became a fact worth stating. A digitized newspaper page, an archive's own finding aid, a library catalog record, a preservation authority's handling guide — each was read, weighed for what kind of evidence it actually is, and only then allowed to support a claim. Where two sources agreed, the claim stands with more confidence. Where they didn't, this exhibit chose to preserve the disagreement rather than quietly pick a side — as it does with the date of Ewing's own marriage25and with when Fayetteville's Western Cabinet newspaper actually began.29That habit of showing the seams is not a weakness in the evidence; it is the evidence behaving honestly.

What follows are the sources carrying the most weight in that process — not a complete list, which lives in the Bibliography, but the ones worth meeting by name.

Voices from 1827 Itself

Two documents in this research came from the very year the Lectures were printed, in the government's own words. President John Quincy Adams's Third Annual Message to Congress, delivered December 4, 1827, is the direct source for this exhibit's picture of the nation the book was born into — a country turned toward commerce, public debt, and the unglamorous machinery of roads and harbors.47, 6A printed congressional memorial from Edgefield, South Carolina, that same year, protesting the tariff debate already stirring, is primary evidence that the sectional strain later called the Tariff of Abominations was already audible in 1827.48

Where the Manuscripts Live

Some of the strongest evidence here isn't a text so much as a map to where the real papers are kept. Brigham Young University's L. Tom Perry Special Collections holds a finding aid for a manuscript collection cataloged as the Finis Ewing collection — correspondence, legal papers, and, by the archive's own description, a reference copy of the 1827 Lectures itself.49The Historical Foundation of the Cumberland Presbyterian Church and CPCA, headquartered in Cordova, Tennessee, is the denomination's own institutional archive, holding Synod minutes, congregational records, and sermons — the first place any future research on Ewing or the Synod's own decision to print his book should look.50A finding aid at the University of Missouri–St. Louis's Mercantile Library describes a collection of Hill family letters, the strongest lead yet located for the print shop's own business correspondence.51And the Smithsonian's Archives Center holds a large body of Baltimore and Ohio Railroad records beginning in 1827 itself, the archival backbone behind this exhibit's account of the transportation age the book narrowly predates.52

Catalogs and Library Records

Library catalogs supplied some of the exhibit's most concrete dates. HathiTrust holds catalog records for two later Hill almanacs — one for 1833, one for 1840 — confirming the Fayetteville printing firm kept operating, and expanding, for years after Ewing's book left its press.53, 54Vanderbilt University's Special Collections catalogs a same-year item, Hills' Tennessee Almanac and State Register, 1827, placing the Hill firm's other work in the identical year as the Lectures.55And a Presbyterian Historical Society finding aid for the American Sunday School Union's records, spanning 1817 to 1915, helped this exhibit place the Lectures inside a wider American habit of printing religious instruction in the 1820s.56, 57

The Authorities Behind the Physical Care

A different kind of source underwrites this exhibit's language about the book as a physical object. The Library of Congress's Preservation Directorate and its research guides on paper deterioration and book repair, the University of Illinois's Preservation Self-Assessment Program guide to paper identification, and the Northeast Document Conservation Center's own preservation leaflets on handling and treating bound materials of value are not stories about Ewing at all — they are the standing authorities this exhibit leaned on to describe rag paper, leather bindings, and how an old book like this one deserves to be handled.58, 59, 60, 61, 62, 63, 30, 40

Related artifacts

Related Artifacts

This book did not survive alone. A small family of objects sits beside it — another physical copy, a shelf of correspondence, an earlier document Ewing himself helped write, and a run of denominational biography written by the people who knew him or followed him. None of these are duplicates of the Lectures; each is a different kind of neighbor.

A Sister Copy, Eight Hundred Miles West

Brigham Young University's L. Tom Perry Special Collections holds a manuscript collection cataloged as the Finis Ewing collection.49Built around roughly forty-eight items of Ewing's own Missouri-years correspondence and legal papers, the collection was later supplemented by the archive's own staff with a photocopy of Cossitt's 1853 biography of Ewing and — the detail that matters most here — a reference copy of the 1827 Lectures itself.49That makes BYU's holding the closest institutional comparator located for this project: a second cataloged copy of the exact book, sitting alongside Ewing's own papers rather than in a bookseller's inventory. Access to the original manuscripts there is restricted to scholars who can show a need beyond the available photocopies.49

It is fair to ask why a Latter-day Saint university holds a Cumberland Presbyterian minister's papers at all. Part of the answer is bibliographic ordinariness — collections migrate, get donated, get cataloged where a family or executor happens to send them. But the Ewing correspondence in this particular collection connects him to Missouri political figures, including Governor Lilburn W. Boggs, for reasons connected to events in 1830s Missouri.49This exhibit does not go further than that. What role, if any, Ewing personally played in those events has not been independently corroborated in this project's research and is deliberately left unstated here.

Worth noting plainly: the finding aid's full wording has not been directly confirmed — the underlying archive page returned an access error to this project's own research pass, so the description above rests on secondhand summaries of that page rather than a clean read of the original.49

A Confession Ewing Helped Draft, Thirteen Years Earlier

Long before the Lectures, Ewing had already put his name to a shared doctrinal document. In 1814, the Cumberland Synod appointed a four-minister committee — William McGee, Finis Ewing, Thomas Calhoun, and Robert Donnell — to prepare a fuller statement of belief, and after a synod-ordered day of fasting and prayer, the committee worked through the Westminster Confession of Faith item by item, ratifying the result unanimously at Sugg's Creek Church, Wilson County, Tennessee, in April 1814.118Where the 1827 Lectures is Ewing's own individual exposition of that faith, the 1814 Confession is the collectively drafted document underneath it — the closest thing this project has found to a direct authorial ancestor of the book.

The Confession did not stay fixed in time: it was revised for governmental structure in 1829, underwent a full doctrinal revision in 1883, and was jointly revised again with the Cumberland Presbyterian Church in America in 1983–84 — a nearly two-century afterlife of active reprinting that the 1827 Lectures itself never had.119One honest gap remains: no source located in this research identifies who printed the very first standalone edition of the 1814 Confession, or whether any copy of that first printing survives anywhere today.118

Aunt Peggy — A Memoir from the Family

In 1873, five years after Margaret Davidson Ewing's death, one of her and Finis Ewing's own sons published Aunt Peggy, a book-length memoir of his mother, through the Cumberland Presbyterian Board of Publication in Nashville.75It survives in full digitized text via a Columbia University Libraries copy on the Internet Archive, but has not yet been read closely for this project.75Of everything on this list, it is the most literal sibling object: written by Ewing's own family, issued by the same denominational press that published his other memorials, in the same commemorative genre.

A Shelf of Founders' Memory

Ewing's own contemporaries and successors wrote about him and each other for decades after his death. Franceway Ranna Cossitt's 1853 biography — the source this exhibit leans on most heavily for Ewing's life — anchors a small cluster of similar work.77, 78Richard Beard, a second-generation Cumberland Presbyterian minister-theologian, included a chapter on Ewing in his 1867 Brief Biographical Sketches of Some of the Early Ministers of the Cumberland Presbyterian Church, and followed it with a Second Series in 1874.120, 121Robert Donnell — Ewing's own colleague on the 1814 Confession committee — published Thoughts on Various Subjects in 1854, including a first-person historical sketch of the church in his own words.122And David Lowry's 1867 biography of Donnell was printed, notably, in Alton, Illinois, rather than through the Nashville or Louisville presses that issued the rest of this cluster.123Read together, these five books look less like scattered biography and more like a young denomination writing its own memory, one founder at a time.

The Nearest Physical Twin

This exhibit's own account of the book's physical form — its likely size, its leather binding, its gilt tooling — is drawn from a comparator, not from this copy: another surviving 1827 Lectures that came up for sale at auction in 2026.31It remains the best external stand-in for what this book's own binding might look like, once examined.

Also from the Hill Press

The Fayetteville shop that printed the Lectures left other surviving traces of its work: The Village Messenger, the town's first lasting newspaper; a line of Hill family almanacs, including one dated to 1827 itself, the same year as the Lectures; and an 1831 Cumberland Presbyterian hymnal.19, 55, 105That hymnal is listed today by the denomination's own Historical Foundation as a title it still needs — meaning the Hill press's hymn book may be harder to find now than the sermon volume this exhibit documents.105

This exhibit was researched, verified, and assembled as a gift — an attempt to hand back a two-hundred-year-old book with its evidence attached, rather than just its age.

How the research was handled

The method was simple to state and slower to practice: prefer a primary source over a secondary one, and a secondary source over a rumor. Every factual sentence here is traceable to a specific source or record, marked so a reader — or a future researcher — can follow it back. Where the record disagreed with itself, that disagreement was kept rather than smoothed away: the exact date of Ewing's own marriage is still an open question across three sources, the founding year of Fayetteville's Western Cabinet newspaper is contested, the identity of "E. and J. B. Hill" as printers is treated as probable rather than certain, and the deepest conflict of all — the church's memory of Ewing against the census record of him as a slaveholder — is told with both accounts standing, unresolved, rather than resolved by whichever made the better story. Confidence itself was tracked claim by claim: some things here are as certain as a title page; others are honestly marked as leads, still open, still waiting on a document nobody has read yet.

That last part is not a flaw to apologize for. A book this old deserves an account of it that admits what it doesn't know as clearly as what it does.

The next chapter of this research is not a document at all — it is the book itself, in hand: its own title page and binding, photographed; its own pages, turned and read; whatever this particular copy has to say that no comparator ever could. That part belongs to its owner now.
